# Worldline

Worldline is a simple way to scan important headlines from around the world. Choose a country, browse its local news edition, and move through the past seven days without leaving the page.

The site is designed for quick context rather than endless scrolling. It brings together headlines from multiple news publishers, groups closely related coverage, and links each story back to the original article.

## What you can do

- Browse 14 country editions: United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, Germany, France, Japan, South Korea, India, Singapore, Spain, Italy, Brazil, and Qatar.
- Start with the United States edition or switch to another country at any time.
- View headlines for today, yesterday, and the five preceding local dates.
- See dates and article times in the selected country's local time.
- Open the original publisher's article directly from each headline.
- Load more stories as you continue reading.
- Use the site comfortably on phones, tablets, and desktops.
- Navigate with a keyboard and use the site with reduced motion settings.

## A clear view of the news

Worldline presents a lead story followed by a broader list of headlines. Similar coverage is kept together where possible so the page is easier to scan, while different publishers remain represented.

The site shows when an edition is current, partially updated, carried over from a previous update, empty, unavailable, or still using sample preview data. This makes gaps in coverage visible instead of presenting them as complete news coverage.

Headlines, publisher names, publication times, and available images are shown as story metadata. Full article text is not reproduced on Worldline; links take you to the publisher's original page.

## Current coverage

Coverage varies by country and date. Some publishers provide more headlines, images, or precise publication times than others, and a source may occasionally be unavailable. Worldline keeps the archive intentionally limited to seven days, so it is best suited to following recent news rather than researching older events.

Some headlines may appear in their original language. English translations may be available for selected stories, but original headlines remain accessible and automated translations may not be perfect.

## About the project

Worldline is an independent headline reader. It does not replace the reporting of the publishers it links to, and its story order is only a reading aid, not a judgment about importance, accuracy, or truth.

Headlines and images belong to their respective publishers. Publishers can request source removal by opening an issue in this repository and identifying the relevant source.
